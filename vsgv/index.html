i. The Thesis

Risk the minimum.
Prove what's real.
Keep playing.

Luke and Jillian both want to work on entrepreneurial ideas — and both want to put the minimum amount of capital at risk to prove whether any given venture is actually possible, and actually works.

VSGV is the vehicle for that experimentation. One structure that lets us keep working on new things on the back of both successes and failures — instead of wiring every idea to a brand-new company from scratch.

01
Minimum stake.
Find out cheap, before it's expensive.
02
One vehicle.
The deal stays. The bets change.
03
Long game.
Both wins and losses buy the next at-bat.
ii. Proposed Model

The studio model —
one vehicle, many bets.

Mirrors the shape Luke runs today at GreenShield Ventures, and is building toward at Stripmall Ventures. Debt in, equity out, the deal stays intact across wins and losses.

01
Rich Partner lends.
Jill wires $100k to VSGV Corp. Sits on the balance sheet as debt — not equity.
→
02
Market validation.
The capital funds research, prototypes, and supply outreach until we pick a winner.
→
03
Sub-company incorporates.
A new entity (e.g. Hot Girls, Inc.) is formed. VSGV owns 100% on day one.
→
04
Debt converts at $1M.
Jill's $100k converts to shares in the sub at a $1M valuation — 10% direct ownership.
Hot Girls, Inc. — post-spin cap table
VSGV 60%
ESOP 30%
Jill 10%
  • VSGV holds 60% — the studio's stake in the venture. Luke and Jill each own half of VSGV.
  • ESOP reserves up to 30% — options for the operators who actually run Hot Girls.
  • Jill, direct gets 10% — $100k ÷ $1M = 10%.
Effective ownership: Jill 40% (30% through VSGV + 10% direct)  ·  Luke 30% (through VSGV)  ·  ESOP 30%.
If a venture fails Jill keeps the full $100k as debt on VSGV's balance sheet, serviced before any distributions from future ventures. The vehicle keeps playing; the capital stays protected.

v. First Product

Hot Girls.

An adhesive body-warmer for the woman who already owns the coat — and still wants to be warm under the dress. 500-year-old Japanese tech (Kairo), reformulated for the fashion-conscious American woman, in three climates.

01 — Mild
Summer in California.
For the woman who runs cold indoors. Built for over-chilled offices, first-class seats, and dinners at a Nobu with the AC on high.
~40°C avg4–6 h
02 — Moderate
Fall in the Hamptons.
Shoulder-season warmth. Sits under knitwear and silk alike — keeps the shiver out of a dinner jacket without bulk.
~50°C avg8–10 h
03 — Full output
Winter in Jackson Hole.
The heavy hitter. High-output exotherm for real cold — tailored for the woman who skis at noon and dines in cashmere at seven.
~60°C avg12+ h
Not The Heat Company (outdoor, sport). Not Thermacare (medical, back pain). Not HotHands (commodity, ski lift). Hot Girls is the first warmer engineered for daily wear under clothing, at premium price, for an audience nobody in the US is talking to.
Why now
Kairo is a $500M+ category in Japan and functionally unknown in America. Zero US brands target the fashion-conscious woman. The audience is obvious, the tech is proven, the packaging is an open door.
